## Internal Analysis Before Question Generation

5. The likely consequence if the problem remains unsolved (what is at stake)

## Core Behavior Focus Rule

Before generating questions, infer the respondent's core real-world behavior
from the business_summary and target persona.

Do NOT default to asking about:
- the product category itself
- the tool or app itself
- the process of preparing to use the product
- meta-work such as planning, organizing, or creating templates

Instead, identify the real job, workflow, or situation the respondent experiences
BEFORE they would ever need this product.
Questions must investigate that real-world behavior.

For every question, focus on:
- what the respondent actually tried to do
- what happened the last time they did it
- where they got stuck
- what they did next
- what workaround they used
- what the consequence was
- whether this pattern repeated across recent situations

Examples of correct focus:
- Product helps with customer interviews
  → Ask about the last time they tried to learn from customers.
  → NOT about preparing interview guides.
- Product helps with marketing posts
  → Ask about the last time they tried to attract users.
  → NOT about writing content.
- Product helps with compliance
  → Ask about the last time they had to complete an audit.
  → NOT about managing documents.
- Product helps with hiring
  → Ask about the last time they tried to find and evaluate candidates.
  → NOT about writing job descriptions.

Always apply this focus BEFORE thinking about tools or workflows:
first understand the real-world situation and behavior,
then (later in the interview) uncover their messy workarounds and alternatives.

## Session 1 Required Learning Flow

For Session 1, generate questions in this order:

1. Recent context:
   Ask about the last time the respondent experienced the situation
   related to the core job.
2. Actual workflow:
   Ask them to walk through what they actually did step by step.
3. Pain event:
   Ask about a specific moment when something felt difficult,
   frustrating, slow, or broken.
4. Consequence:
   Ask what happened because of that problem — time lost, money lost,
   delayed decision, stress, missed opportunity.
5. Workaround:
   Ask what they actually did to get around the problem.
6. Alternative usage:
   Ask about the last time they tried another tool, template,
   service, or manual process.
7. Switch trigger:
   Ask what happened when they realized the current workaround
   was not enough.

### Session 1 Flow Compression Rule

If only 6 questions are generated for Session 1:
- Merge "Pain event" and "Consequence" into one question, OR
- Merge "Alternative usage" and "Switch trigger" into one question.
Keep the learning flow intact.
Prefer fewer deeper questions over many shallow ones.

## Banned Question Patterns (NEVER use these in Session 1)

- "What would need to be different for you to..." (hypothetical future)
- "What would it be worth to you if..." (pricing anchor)
- "What would need to be true for you to..." (hypothetical condition)
- "If you had a magic wand..." or any Magic Wand style questions
- "How often do you..." (frequency estimate — use past event anchor instead)
- "What's your current approach to..." (generic — use specific past event instead)
- Any question starting with "Would you..." or "Could you imagine..."
- Any hypothetical "what if this existed?" scenario
- Any question about price, value, willingness to pay, or budget

## Required Question Patterns (ALL questions must use these forms)

Every question must be anchored to a specific past event or
concrete experience:
- "Tell me about the last time you..."
- "Walk me through what happened when..."
- "Think back to a recent situation where... What did you do?"
- "Describe a specific moment when..."
- "What did you actually do the last time...?"
- "Can you give me a specific example of when...?"

### Severity and recurrence (Session 1)

When you need to understand how often or how strongly a problem shows up,
NEVER ask direct frequency estimates like "How often do you...?"

Instead use:
- "Tell me about another recent time when this happened. What was going on?"
- "Can you recall the last 2 or 3 times this happened?
   What did you actually do each time?"
- "Think about the most painful time this happened recently.
   What made that moment particularly bad?"

### Recency window

Unless the business_summary requires a different timeframe:
- Treat "recent" as within the last 3~6 months.
- Prefer questions answerable with a concrete story
  from the last 3~6 months.
- If needed, say "recently (in the last few months)" in the question.

## Activation Friction Rule

If (and only if) the business_summary clearly shows that the product
requires the user to take a real-world activation action,
include ONE question that investigates the last time they hesitated,
delayed, or avoided a similar action.

If the product does not involve any clear activation action,
you MAY skip this rule and instead add one more question about
the core workflow or pain event.

Activation actions include:
- sending a link or invitation
- uploading a file
- connecting an account
- inviting a teammate
- publishing content
- contacting a customer
- sharing sensitive information
- asking someone for feedback

The question MUST be anchored to a past event.

Good:
"Tell me about the last time you needed to send something to a customer
 to get feedback. What made you hesitate or delay it?"
Bad:
"Would you feel comfortable sending this link?"

## Alternatives Section Clarification

For the "alternatives" section
(after you have already understood the real context and pain event):
- Always try to surface what the respondent actually did instead of
  solving the problem perfectly.
- Valid alternatives include: using another tool, using a spreadsheet,
  doing it manually, asking a colleague, or even doing nothing and
  living with the problem.
- Make it safe for respondents to admit "messy" workarounds or that
  they just ignored the problem.

## Icebreaker Rules

- Always generate exactly 3 icebreakers
- All 3 must flow naturally as one opening monologue
- First: warm greeting + ask respondent's job/role AND where they're based,
  combined into one natural question (intent: "rapport_building")
  → MUST ask both: what they do for work AND where they're located
  → This question is always fixed as the first icebreaker
- Second: acknowledge the respondent's answer in a generic or
  placeholder-safe way, empathize with their situation,
  and help them feel relaxed.
  Do NOT invent details about their role, location, company,
  or experience.
  Use neutral wording such as "That's helpful context"
  instead of making specific claims.
  (intent: "role_context")
- Third: naturally signal the start of the interview,
  create psychological safety, keep it light and brief.
  (intent: "transition")
- Each icebreaker: 2~3 sentences max
- Never mention: solutions, products, or repeat formal greetings
- All icebreakers: section = "icebreaker", report_bucket = "context"

Example (good):
- "Great to meet you! Could you tell me what you do for work
   and where you're based?"
- "That's helpful context. I'd love to understand what your
   day-to-day looks like around this topic."
- "There are no right or wrong answers here — I'm just here to listen
   and learn from your real experience. Let's dive in."

## Required Components Per Question

Each question must include:

1. Question text (past-behavior form ONLY)
2. Why this question? (1~3 sentences, linked to lean customer
   development principles)
3. 2~3 follow-up hints (short prompts that dig deeper —
   plain strings, not objects)

Follow-up hints must be specific probing prompts:
- "What did you do right after that happened?"
- "How did that make you feel in that moment?"
- "What was the impact on your work/team/business?"

## Examples (Session 1 — Problem Interview)

Bad question (do NOT copy this style):
"Would you use a tool that automatically interviewed your customers
 and sent you a report?"

Why it's bad:
- Hypothetical ("would you")
- Mentions a solution/tool directly
- Asks for opinion about the future, not past behavior

Good question (copy this style instead):
"Tell me about the last time you needed to understand what your
 customers wanted and did not end up talking to them directly.
 What happened?"

Why it's good:
- Anchored in a specific past situation
- Focuses on real behavior and constraints
- No mention of any tool, product, or future solution
- Does not pre-assign a reason for avoidance

Follow-up hints:
- "What was happening that made you feel you needed that insight?"
- "What did you try first, before talking to anyone?"
- "What was the impact of handling it that way?"
