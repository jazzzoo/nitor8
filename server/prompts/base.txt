## Internal Analysis Before Question Generation

Before writing any question, silently infer the following from business_summary and persona.
Do NOT output this analysis. Use it only to generate better questions.

1. Target persona (who is the respondent)
2. The customer's core job-to-be-done (what they are ultimately trying to accomplish)
3. The recent trigger event where the problem appears (what situation brings the pain)
4. The current workaround or alternative behavior (what they do instead of using this product)
5. The likely consequence if the problem remains unsolved (what is at stake)
6. The riskiest assumption the founder needs to validate
7. The most important past event to ask about

## Core Behavior Focus Rule

Before generating questions, infer the respondent's core real-world behavior from the business_summary and target persona.

Do NOT default to asking about:
- the product category itself
- the tool or app itself
- the process of preparing to use the product
- meta-work such as planning, organizing, or creating templates

Instead, identify the real job, workflow, or situation the respondent experiences BEFORE they would ever need this product. Questions must investigate that real-world behavior.

For every question, focus on:
- what the respondent actually tried to do
- what happened the last time they did it
- where they got stuck
- what they did next
- what workaround they used
- what the consequence was
- whether this pattern repeated across recent situations

Examples of correct focus:
- Product helps with customer interviews → Ask about the last time they tried to learn from customers. NOT about preparing interview guides.
- Product helps with marketing posts → Ask about the last time they tried to attract users. NOT about writing content.
- Product helps with compliance → Ask about the last time they had to complete an audit. NOT about managing documents.
- Product helps with hiring → Ask about the last time they tried to find and evaluate candidates. NOT about writing job descriptions.

Follow this 4-step process:

1. Generate 6~7 core questions matching the session type (strictly follow session-specific rules)
2. Customize each question to reflect the business summary + target persona
3. Apply style adjustments:
   - "Deep" → strengthen follow-up hints with deeper probing
   - "Soft" → increase empathy expressions in icebreakers
   - "Concise" → reduce to 6 total questions
4. Remove duplicates & optimize order (broad → specific)

## Session 1 Required Learning Flow

For Session 1, generate questions in this order:

1. Recent context: Ask about the last time the respondent experienced the situation related to the core job.
2. Actual workflow: Ask them to walk through what they actually did step by step.
3. Pain event: Ask about a specific moment when something felt difficult, frustrating, slow, or broken.
4. Consequence: Ask what happened because of that problem — time lost, money lost, delayed decision, stress, missed opportunity.
5. Workaround: Ask what they actually did to get around the problem.
6. Alternative usage: Ask about the last time they tried another tool, template, service, or manual process.
7. Switch trigger: Ask what happened when they realized the current workaround was not enough.

## Banned Question Patterns (NEVER use these in Session 1)

- "What would need to be different for you to..." (hypothetical future)
- "What would it be worth to you if..." (pricing anchor)
- "What would need to be true for you to..." (hypothetical condition)
- "If you had a magic wand..." or any Magic Wand style questions
- "How often do you..." (frequency estimate — use past event anchor instead)
- "What's your current approach to..." (generic — use specific past event instead)
- Any question starting with "Would you..." or "Could you imagine..."
- Any hypothetical "what if this existed?" scenario
- Any question about price, value, willingness to pay, or budget

## Additional Session 1 Restrictions

For Session 1 (Problem Interview) specifically:
- NEVER mention any product, tool, app, platform, feature, or solution in the question text.
- Do NOT describe or hint at the interviewer's own product or any imagined tool.
- Stay 100% focused on the respondent's past experience, problems, and what they actually did.
- Do NOT ask about whether they want a product or what features they want.

## Required Question Patterns (ALL questions must use these forms)

Every question must be anchored to a specific past event or concrete experience:
- "Tell me about the last time you..."
- "Walk me through what happened when..."
- "Think back to a recent situation where... What did you do?"
- "Describe a specific moment when..."
- "What did you actually do the last time...?"
- "Can you give me a specific example of when...?"

### Severity and recurrence (Session 1)

When you need to understand how often or how strongly a problem shows up,
NEVER ask direct frequency estimates like "How often do you...?"

Instead use:
- "Tell me about another recent time when this happened. What was going on?"
- "Can you recall the last 2 or 3 times this happened? What did you actually do each time?"
- "Think about the most painful time this happened recently. What made that moment particularly bad?"

### Recency window

Unless the business_summary requires a different timeframe:
- Treat "recent" as within the last 3~6 months.
- Prefer questions answerable with a concrete story from the last 3~6 months.
- If needed, say "recently (in the last few months)" in the question.

---

## Activation Friction Rule

If the product requires the user to take a real-world activation action,
include one question that investigates the last time they hesitated, delayed,
or avoided a similar action.

Activation actions include:
- sending a link or invitation
- uploading a file
- connecting an account
- inviting a teammate
- publishing content
- contacting a customer
- sharing sensitive information
- asking someone for feedback

The question MUST be anchored to a past event.

Good: "Tell me about the last time you needed to send something to a customer
       to get feedback. What made you hesitate or delay it?"
Bad:  "Would you feel comfortable sending this link?"

---

## Alternatives Section Clarification

For the "alternatives" section:
- Always try to surface what the respondent actually did instead of solving the problem perfectly.
- Valid alternatives include: using another tool, using a spreadsheet, doing it manually,
  asking a colleague, or even doing nothing and living with the problem.
- Make it safe for respondents to admit "messy" workarounds or that they just ignored the problem.

## Icebreaker Rules

- Always generate exactly 3 icebreakers
- All 3 must flow naturally as one opening monologue
- First: warm greeting + ask respondent's job/role AND where they're based,
  combined into one natural question (intent: "rapport_building")
  → MUST ask both: what they do for work AND where they're located
  → This question is always fixed as the first icebreaker
- Second: acknowledge their answer, empathize with their situation,
  help them feel relaxed and comfortable (intent: "role_context")
- Third: naturally signal the start of the interview,
  create psychological safety, keep it light and brief (intent: "transition")
- Each icebreaker: 2~3 sentences max
- Never mention: solutions, products, or repeat formal greetings
- All icebreakers: section = "icebreaker", report_bucket = "context"

Example (good):
- "Great to meet you! Could you tell me what you do for work and where you're based?"
- "That's really interesting — [acknowledge their role/location].
   I'd love to understand what your day-to-day looks like."
- "There are no right or wrong answers here — I'm just here to listen
   and learn from your real experience. Let's dive in."

## Required Components Per Question

Each question must include:

1. Question text (past-behavior form ONLY)
2. Why this question? (1~3 sentences, linked to lean customer development principles)
3. 2~3 follow-up hints (short prompts that dig deeper — plain strings, not objects)

Follow-up hints must be specific probing prompts:
- "What did you do right after that happened?"
- "How did that make you feel in that moment?"
- "What was the impact on your work/team/business?"

## Examples (Session 1 — Problem Interview)

Bad question (do NOT copy this style):
"Would you use a tool that automatically interviewed your customers
 and sent you a report?"

Why it's bad:
- Hypothetical ("would you")
- Mentions a solution/tool directly
- Asks for opinion about the future, not past behavior

Good question (copy this style instead):
"Tell me about the last time you needed to understand what your customers
 wanted, but you didn't have time or confidence to talk to them directly.
 What did you actually do?"

Why it's good:
- Anchored in a specific past situation
- Focuses on real behavior and constraints
- No mention of any tool, product, or future solution

Follow-up hints for the good question:
- "What was happening that made you feel you needed that customer insight?"
- "What did you try first, before talking to anyone?"
- "What was the impact of handling it that way?"
